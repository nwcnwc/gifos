How this meeting works

This meeting has no size limit. Two people or two million, it runs the same. The dream it is built for: the whole Earth in one meeting at once — everyone able to speak, everyone sharing one live room. It scales by folding the crowd into itself, so your phone only ever talks to a handful of neighbors no matter how vast the room grows.

The stadium — how a giant room is arranged

Picture a stadium. You don't meet a million faces; you meet the few around you, and the rest is a living backdrop.

  • Seat — one person.
  • Stage — the front. Whoever steps up here is seen and heard first by everyone in the whole stadium (the reader, a speaker, a shared app).
  • Row — the handful of people right next to you. This is your real conversation — you're directly connected to them.
  • Section — the rows near you. You see them as a live mosaic, not as a wall of individual faces.
  • The stadium — everyone beyond your section, folded down into a shimmering backdrop and a single summed roar. A million-voice “Amen” reaches you as one sound.
  • Deacon — each row quietly elects one phone to gather its row into a single tidy tile and pass it along. It happens automatically; you never manage it.

Nobody has to know how big the room is. Each phone follows the same simple rules, and the structure just grows — the way a crowd naturally forms rows and sections without anyone directing traffic.

The screen — one endless scroll

Top to bottom, nearest first: the stage, then your row, then the rest of your section — the folded rows around you — and finally the far stadium, the other sections and decks, folded smaller the farther out they sit. In a room that fits one section, the mosaic simply is your section; the far stadium only appears once the room grows past it. Keep scrolling to wander outward. Tap any folded tile to zoom it larger; tap again to fold it back.

The sound mix — four faders that grow in

Tap Mix for your own private sound board (it only changes what you hear — like your own volume knob). Faders appear as the room gets big enough to need them:

  • Stage — the front of the room.
  • My row — the people right beside you.
  • My section — the near-field murmur around you (appears once there's more than your row).
  • The stadium — the far roar of everyone else (appears once the room spans past your section). Turn it down during a quiet reading, then push it up to let the whole crowd's response wash in.

The buttons worth knowing

  • Blur (Max / Min / None) — how blurred your own video looks to everyone. You always control your own camera; nobody can un-blur you.
  • Stage — step up to be seen and heard first by the whole room; tap again to step down.
  • Hand — raise it and you float to the top of a room-wide queue, in order.
  • Password — the key to clear video. Set one and faces sharpen for those who have it; without one, everyone stays gently blurred.
  • Invite — one link brings people straight in.
  • Run app — share an app (a Bible, a board, a game) that everyone in the room uses together, live.
  • Record / CC — record to your own device, or turn on live captions your phone writes from your own voice.
  • Admin — some rooms have a host who can moderate; most rooms are open and run on friendly anarchy.

Your privacy

Video and audio go straight between people, never through a server. Because of that, everyone on a call can see everyone's network address — nobody here is anonymous (tap the status text to see the list). It's the honest trade for having no middleman.
